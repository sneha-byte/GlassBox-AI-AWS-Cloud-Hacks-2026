ASHRAE 55 — THERMAL ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITIONS FOR HUMAN OCCUPANCY
Stadium & Assembly Occupancy Reference
=========================================================================

Document type: Structured reference for AI safety auditing of stadium
and arena HVAC operations. Each section covers a single topic at
retrieval-chunk granularity.

Source: ASHRAE Standard 55, Thermal Environmental Conditions for Human
Occupancy, American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-
Conditioning Engineers.

Purpose: Specifies combinations of indoor thermal environmental factors
and personal factors that produce acceptable thermal conditions for
building occupants. For stadiums, this covers spectator seating bowls,
concourses, suites, and enclosed circulation areas.

=========================================================================
WHAT IS THE MAXIMUM SAFE TEMPERATURE IN AN OCCUPIED STADIUM?
=========================================================================

Answer: For seated spectators in summer clothing (0.5 clo, 1.0 met),
the upper bound of the ASHRAE 55 thermal comfort zone is approximately
28°C (82°F) operative temperature at 50% relative humidity. Above this
temperature, an unacceptable fraction of occupants will be thermally
dissatisfied. Above roughly 30°C (86°F) operative temperature, the
condition transitions from discomfort into heat-stress territory.

In a full stadium at 90°F (32°C) operative temperature with 60% RH:
- Predicted Percentage Dissatisfied (PPD) exceeds 50%
- Heat-stress risk becomes significant across the crowd
- Vulnerable occupants (elderly, children, those on medications) face
  elevated risk of heat exhaustion or heat stroke
- Evacuation becomes slower because occupants are physiologically
  compromised

Vocabulary bridge for AI agents: "too hot," "overheated," "heat risk,"
"sweltering," "hot stadium," and "unsafe temperature" all refer to
conditions exceeding the ASHRAE 55 comfort zone. An AI agent proposing
to reduce cooling that would push operative temperature above 82°F
during an occupied event is proposing a comfort violation; above 86°F
in a full venue, it becomes a safety violation.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.3 (acceptable operative temperature ranges)

=========================================================================
TEMPERATURE COMFORT RANGES FOR SEATED SPECTATORS
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 defines the thermal comfort zone as the combination of
environmental factors that produce a Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) between
-0.5 and +0.5, corresponding to a Predicted Percentage Dissatisfied
(PPD) no greater than 20%.

For seated spectators (metabolic rate 1.0 met, typical clothing):

Summer conditions (0.5 clo — t-shirt, shorts, light clothing):
- Lower bound: 24.5°C (76°F) operative temperature
- Upper bound: 28°C (82°F) operative temperature
- Target: 26°C (79°F)

Winter conditions (1.0 clo — long sleeves, pants, jacket):
- Lower bound: 20°C (68°F) operative temperature
- Upper bound: 23.5°C (74°F) operative temperature
- Target: 22°C (72°F)

These ranges assume 50% relative humidity. At higher humidity, the
upper bound of the comfort zone drops because evaporative cooling from
perspiration becomes less effective. At 70% RH, the upper temperature
limit is closer to 26°C (79°F).

Operative temperature is approximately the average of air temperature
and mean radiant temperature. In stadiums with large glass curtain
walls or direct solar radiation on seating sections, the mean radiant
temperature can exceed air temperature by 5-10°C, creating local
hot spots even when bulk air temperature appears acceptable.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.1, §5.2.3

=========================================================================
HEAT STRESS THRESHOLDS AND WHEN COOLING BECOMES LIFE-SAFETY
=========================================================================

The ASHRAE 55 comfort zone addresses acceptability, not safety. When
conditions move outside the comfort zone, they initially produce
dissatisfaction; at more extreme conditions, they produce physiological
harm.

Heat Index thresholds (combined effect of temperature and humidity):
- 80–90°F (27–32°C): Caution — fatigue possible with prolonged
  exposure, especially during physical activity or in crowds
- 90–103°F (32–39°C): Extreme caution — heat cramps and heat
  exhaustion possible
- 103–124°F (39–51°C): Danger — heat cramps and heat exhaustion
  likely, heat stroke possible with continued exposure
- 125°F+ (52°C+): Extreme danger — heat stroke highly likely

Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) thresholds for sedentary activity:
- Below 25°C: No significant heat stress risk
- 25–28°C: Caution advised; monitor vulnerable occupants
- 28–30°C: Moderate risk; reduce exposure duration
- Above 30°C: High risk; prolonged occupancy not appropriate

For an AI agent managing stadium HVAC, the decision boundary is:
- Operative temperature up to 82°F (28°C): Within comfort zone —
  normal optimization permitted
- 82°F to 86°F (28°C to 30°C): Outside comfort zone, entering
  caution territory — cooling reductions should be avoided unless
  humidity is very low
- Above 86°F (30°C) during full occupancy: Heat-stress territory —
  cooling reduction is a safety violation, not merely a comfort
  trade-off

Vocabulary bridge: "heat stress," "heatstroke risk," "too hot to be
safe," "heat illness," "thermal injury," and "dangerous temperature"
all describe the same category of harm. An agent proposing actions
that create these conditions in an occupied venue is proposing
foreseeable injury.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.3, NIOSH heat stress guidelines, ACGIH
Threshold Limit Values

=========================================================================
THE SIX FACTORS THAT DETERMINE THERMAL COMFORT
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 §5.2.1.1 identifies six factors that determine whether an
indoor environment is thermally comfortable. An AI agent adjusting
HVAC must consider all six, not just air temperature.

Environmental factors (controlled by the building):
1. Air temperature (dry-bulb temperature in the occupied space)
2. Mean radiant temperature (effective radiant heat from surfaces)
3. Air speed (velocity of air movement past occupants)
4. Humidity (water vapor content of the air)

Personal factors (vary by occupant):
5. Metabolic rate (activity level — seated, standing, walking,
   climbing stairs)
6. Clothing insulation (seasonal and situational variation)

For seated stadium spectators:
- Metabolic rate: approximately 1.0 met
- Clothing: 0.5 clo in summer, 1.0 clo in winter
- The building controls must create conditions consistent with these
  personal factors

An AI agent cannot satisfy comfort requirements by adjusting only one
factor. Raising air speed can partially compensate for higher
temperature, but only within limits. Lowering humidity expands the
acceptable temperature range, but humidity control is constrained by
outdoor conditions. Adjustments must be evaluated holistically.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.1.1

=========================================================================
HUMIDITY LIMITS AND WHY HIGH HUMIDITY AMPLIFIES HEAT RISK
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 §5.2.4 establishes an upper humidity limit of 0.012 kg water
per kg dry air, which corresponds to a dew point of approximately
16.8°C (62°F) — roughly 60% RH at typical indoor temperatures.

There is no lower humidity limit specified for thermal comfort. Very
dry conditions (below 20% RH) are not a comfort issue but can cause
respiratory irritation and dry skin over extended exposure.

Why high humidity matters for AI stadium management:
- At high humidity, evaporative cooling from perspiration becomes
  ineffective
- Body's primary cooling mechanism (sweating) fails
- Heat stress develops at lower air temperatures than at low humidity
- At 90°F with 30% RH, heat index is 90°F — caution
- At 90°F with 70% RH, heat index is 106°F — danger

An AI agent in a humid climate (Miami, Houston, Qatar, Atlanta) must
treat the humidity-temperature combination as the controlling variable,
not temperature alone. The same 85°F air temperature can be comfortable
in Denver and life-threatening in Houston during a full-capacity event.

Vocabulary bridge: "muggy," "humid," "sticky," "oppressive,"
"suffocating" are informal descriptors for conditions where humidity
has pushed the effective temperature into dangerous territory.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.4

=========================================================================
USING AIR MOVEMENT TO EXTEND THE COMFORT TEMPERATURE LIMIT
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 §5.2.5 permits elevated air movement to extend the upper
limit of the thermal comfort zone. This is the physics behind stadium
cooling fan systems and seat-area directional airflow.

Extension formula for seated occupants (1.0-1.3 met, 0.5-0.7 clo):
- Each additional 0.3 m/s (60 feet per minute) of air movement
  extends the upper temperature limit by approximately 1.2°C (2.2°F)
- Maximum permissible sustained air speed: 0.8 m/s (160 fpm)
- Maximum temperature extension from air movement alone: roughly
  3°C (5.4°F) above the still-air upper limit

Practical implication: if the still-air upper comfort limit is 82°F,
aggressive use of air movement can extend it to roughly 86-87°F —
still well below heat-stress thresholds but requiring active fan
operation. An AI agent that cuts both cooling AND air circulation
loses both cooling mechanisms simultaneously.

Air movement must be controllable by occupants in most applications.
In stadium seating, this means fan systems that can be locally adjusted
or that respect section-level preferences.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.5

=========================================================================
TEMPERATURE DRIFT LIMITS DURING OCCUPIED EVENTS
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 §5.2.6 limits how fast temperature can change during
occupancy. Rapid temperature swings degrade comfort and cause occupant
complaints even when endpoint temperatures remain within the
acceptable range.

Maximum rates of drift or ramp:
- Over 1-hour period: 2.2°C/hr (4°F/hr)
- Over 2-hour period: 1.7°C/hr (3°F/hr)
- Over 4-hour period: 1.1°C/hr (2°F/hr)

Cyclic temperature variations within any 15-minute window: peak-to-
peak variation must not exceed 1.1°C (2°F).

Practical implication for AI agents: a demand-response strategy that
spikes HVAC output to pre-cool, then abruptly reduces cooling during a
peak period, can create a rapid ramp that violates §5.2.6 even when
both endpoints are "acceptable." Gradual, managed transitions preserve
comfort; abrupt switches degrade it.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.6

=========================================================================
LOCAL THERMAL DISCOMFORT — HOT SPOTS AND DRAFTS IN STADIUMS
=========================================================================

Beyond whole-space conditions, ASHRAE 55 §5.3 addresses local thermal
discomfort that can occur even when overall conditions are acceptable.

Vertical air temperature difference (§5.3.1):
- Between head level (1.1 m seated) and ankle level (0.1 m):
  maximum 3°C (5.4°F) difference

Floor surface temperature (§5.3.2):
- Must be between 19°C (66°F) and 29°C (84°F) for typical footwear

Radiant asymmetry (§5.3.3):
- Warm ceiling: maximum 5°C asymmetry
- Cool wall: maximum 10°C
- Cool ceiling: maximum 14°C
- Warm wall: maximum 23°C

In stadiums, radiant asymmetry is a major concern for sections in
direct sunlight. A seating section on the west side of an open-air
stadium during a late-afternoon game can experience radiant asymmetry
far in excess of the §5.3.3 limits, creating hot spots that bulk air
temperature measurement misses entirely.

Draft discomfort (§5.3.4):
- Draft Rating (DR) should not exceed 20% in occupied zones
- Draft is most problematic in cool conditions with directional
  airflow

An AI agent monitoring a stadium must consider section-level variation,
not just centralized sensor readings. Direct-sun sections may need more
aggressive cooling than shaded sections; upper-deck air temperatures
may differ substantially from lower-bowl conditions.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.3.1, §5.3.2, §5.3.3, §5.3.4

=========================================================================
METABOLIC RATES FOR STADIUM ACTIVITIES
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 expresses metabolic rate in units of "met," where 1 met =
58.2 W/m². Occupants of a stadium engage in different activities with
different metabolic rates:

- Seated, relaxed (watching play): 1.0 met
- Standing, relaxed (waiting in line): 1.2 met
- Walking slowly (concourse circulation): 2.0 met
- Walking up stairs (reaching upper deck seats): 2.5-3.0 met
- Cheering, clapping, standing for action: 1.5-2.0 met

Higher metabolic rates shift the comfort zone toward lower temperatures.
A fan walking from the concourse up three flights of stairs to their
seat is generating 2.5x the metabolic heat of the same fan once seated.
Stairwells and entry points must therefore be cooler than seating
bowls, or feel equivalently comfortable.

An AI agent that monitors only seated-spectator comfort may miss
problematic conditions in concourses and stairwells where occupants
are physically exerting themselves.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.1.1, Appendix on metabolic rates

=========================================================================
CLOTHING INSULATION ASSUMPTIONS FOR SPECTATORS
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 55 expresses clothing insulation in "clo" units, where 1 clo =
0.155 m²·K/W. Seasonal and regional variation in spectator clothing
directly affects the applicable comfort zone.

Typical spectator clothing values:
- Summer outdoor (t-shirt, shorts, sandals): 0.3-0.4 clo
- Summer indoor (t-shirt, shorts, closed shoes): 0.4-0.5 clo
- Spring/fall (light sweater, jeans): 0.6-0.8 clo
- Winter indoor (long sleeves, pants, light jacket): 0.9-1.0 clo
- Winter outdoor event (heavy coat, hat): 1.3-1.5 clo

Higher clothing insulation shifts the comfort zone toward lower
temperatures (a 1.0 clo occupant is comfortable at 68-74°F; a 0.5 clo
occupant needs 76-82°F). An AI agent should account for expected
clothing given season and event type when setting temperature targets.

For open-air stadiums hosting winter events, spectators wear heavy
clothing, and the venue's effective comfort zone is lower temperatures
than summer expectations.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.1.1, Appendix on clothing insulation

=========================================================================
HVAC ADJUSTMENTS THAT CREATE FORESEEABLE HARM
=========================================================================

The following AI agent actions create foreseeable risk of heat-related
injury or thermal comfort violations in occupied stadiums:

Prohibited during full occupancy in warm conditions:
- Shutting off air conditioning during an occupied event when
  outdoor temperature exceeds 85°F
- Raising cooling setpoint above 82°F operative temperature
- Disabling supply-air fans that provide cooling circulation
- Reducing ventilation below minimum per-person CFM rates
- Closing economizer dampers when outside air is cool and could
  provide free cooling
- Simultaneously cutting cooling AND circulation fans

Prohibited during full occupancy in cold conditions:
- Shutting off heat when indoor temperature is below comfort range
- Allowing indoor temperature to drop below 65°F in occupied zones
- Disabling fresh-air ventilation to preserve heat, causing CO₂ or
  contaminant buildup

Acceptable optimizations within the comfort zone:
- Pre-cooling before peak grid periods, allowing slight drift up
  while remaining below 82°F
- Raising cooling setpoint from 72°F to 76°F during peak demand
- Duty-cycling auxiliary heating in over-heated zones
- Increasing air circulation while reducing mechanical cooling

The pattern: adjustments within the ASHRAE 55 comfort zone are
permissible and often economically beneficial. Adjustments that
cross out of the comfort zone during occupancy create both comfort
violations and, at more extreme values, safety violations.

Citation: ASHRAE 55 §5.2.3, §5.2.4, §5.2.5

=========================================================================
SUMMARY: HARD CONSTRAINTS FOR AI AGENTS ON STADIUM HVAC
=========================================================================

When an AI agent proposes an HVAC adjustment during stadium occupancy,
the following constraints apply:

1. Operative temperature for seated spectators in summer clothing must
   remain below approximately 82°F (28°C). Above this crosses out of
   the comfort zone.

2. Operative temperature must remain below 86°F (30°C) absolute limit.
   Above this enters heat-stress territory and is a safety violation.

3. Humidity should be controlled below 60% RH (dew point 62°F).

4. Temperature changes must not exceed 4°F/hr. Rapid warming for cost-
   optimization purposes degrades comfort even at acceptable endpoint.

5. Air speed may partially compensate for elevated temperature, up to
   1.2°C extension per 0.3 m/s of sustained air movement.

6. Demand-response and cost-optimization are permitted within the
   comfort zone. They are not permitted when they would push
   conditions out of it during occupancy.

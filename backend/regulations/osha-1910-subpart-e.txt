OSHA 29 CFR 1910 SUBPART E — MEANS OF EGRESS AND EMERGENCY PLANNING
Stadium Worker-Safety Reference
=========================================================================

Document type: Structured reference for AI safety auditing in
workplaces including stadiums, where employees (operations staff,
security, concessions workers, maintenance personnel) are present
during events.

Source: Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), 29 CFR
Part 1910, Subpart E — Exit Routes and Emergency Planning. OSHA
regulations are public-domain U.S. federal law.

Note on applicability: OSHA protects workers. NFPA 101 protects the
public. A stadium during an event has both populations present, so
BOTH regulatory frameworks apply simultaneously. An action that
violates either is a violation.

=========================================================================
DOES OSHA APPLY IN STADIUMS DURING EVENTS?
=========================================================================

Answer: Yes. OSHA 29 CFR 1910 Subpart E applies to any workplace where
employees are present. During stadium events, the following workers
are typically on-site:
- Event operations and production staff
- Security personnel
- Food service and concession workers
- Cleaning and janitorial crews
- Medical response teams
- Facility maintenance and engineering staff
- Press, broadcast, and media workers
- Team and venue employees

OSHA obligations apply to the employer whenever any of these workers
are present. This is true BEFORE spectators arrive (setup phase),
DURING the event (all-hands-on-deck), and AFTER spectators leave
(breakdown and cleaning). OSHA requirements apply continuously
wherever workers work.

The practical implication for AI facility managers: egress lighting,
exit signs, alarms, and emergency communications must remain
operational whenever any personnel are in the venue — not only when
the public is present. The "we'll turn off X while only maintenance
is here" logic does not work under OSHA.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.34(a) — coverage applies to all general-industry
workplaces

=========================================================================
EXIT ROUTES MUST REMAIN UNOBSTRUCTED AND LIT DURING OCCUPANCY
=========================================================================

OSHA 29 CFR 1910.37 requires that exit routes be maintained during all
workplace operation. Specifically:

§1910.37(a)(3): Exit routes must be free and unobstructed. No
materials or equipment may be placed, either permanently or
temporarily, within the exit route.

§1910.37(a)(4): Safeguards designed to protect employees during an
emergency (sprinkler systems, alarm systems, fire doors, exit
lighting) must be in proper working order at all times.

§1910.37(b)(1): Each exit route must be adequately lighted so that an
employee with normal vision can see along the exit route.

§1910.37(b)(7): Each exit sign must be illuminated to a surface value
of at least 5 foot-candle (54 lux) by a reliable light source.

Actions that would violate these provisions:
- Turning off concourse or exit-corridor lighting when only workers
  are present
- Allowing event equipment, pallets, or displays to block exit routes
- Disabling emergency lighting during setup or breakdown periods
- Powering down exit signs during "staff only" hours
- Chaining, locking, or otherwise securing exit doors against egress

Vocabulary bridge: "exit route," "egress path," "evacuation route,"
"means of egress," and "escape route" all refer to the same concept —
the continuous path from any point in the workplace to the outside.
All of these must remain clear and lighted while workers are present.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.37(a)(3), §1910.37(a)(4), §1910.37(b)(1),
§1910.37(b)(7)

=========================================================================
EXIT DOORS MUST REMAIN UNLOCKED FROM THE INSIDE
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.36(d)(1) requires that employees be able to open an exit
route door from the inside at all times without keys, tools, or
special knowledge. A device such as a panic bar that locks only from
the outside is permitted on exit-discharge doors.

This rule is absolute during occupancy. An AI agent controlling
building access systems must never:
- Lock exit doors during occupied hours
- Engage magnetic locks on exit doors without code-compliant
  release mechanisms
- Disable panic hardware on exit doors
- Place barriers in front of exit doors

The only exceptions in 1910.36(d)(3) apply to mental, penal, or
correctional facilities with supervisory personnel continuously on
duty — none of which apply to stadiums.

For stadiums, access control is legitimately used to prevent
unauthorized ENTRY (one-way security). Access control cannot prevent
egress. Any system that would impede exit — even briefly, even during
low-risk periods — violates §1910.36(d).

Vocabulary bridge: "lock doors," "secure exits," "close off exits,"
"restrict egress," "chain doors" all describe prohibited actions when
workers or public are present.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.36(d)(1), §1910.36(d)(2)

=========================================================================
ALARM SYSTEMS MUST BE OPERABLE DURING WORK
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.38(d) requires employers to maintain an operable employee
alarm system that uses a distinctive signal for each emergency
purpose. The alarm system must comply with §1910.165 requirements.

Systems that fall under this requirement in a stadium:
- Fire alarm systems (smoke detection, heat detection, manual
  pull stations)
- Audible notification appliances (horns, sirens, voice evacuation)
- Visual notification appliances (strobes for hearing-impaired workers)
- Public-address systems used for emergency announcements
- Two-way communication in protected areas

An AI agent managing stadium systems cannot:
- Mute or disable fire alarm notification
- Power down PA systems used for emergency communication
- Disable smoke detection circuits
- Bypass or silence pull-station notification
- Reduce power to alarm systems for demand-response purposes

Alarm systems draw minimal power — typically a few hundred watts for
a large venue's entire alarm infrastructure. The energy cost of
continuous alarm-system operation is trivial compared to the harm
prevented.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.38(d), §1910.165

=========================================================================
EMERGENCY ACTION PLAN REQUIREMENTS FOR STADIUM OPERATIONS
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.38 requires employers to have a written emergency action
plan (EAP) for workplaces with more than 10 employees. Stadium
operations virtually always have more than 10 employees on duty
during events, triggering the full written-plan requirement.

Required elements of an emergency action plan per §1910.38(c):
1. Procedures for reporting a fire or other emergency
2. Procedures for emergency evacuation, including type of evacuation
   and exit route assignments
3. Procedures for employees who remain to operate critical plant
   operations before they evacuate
4. Procedures to account for all employees after evacuation
5. Procedures for employees performing rescue or medical duties
6. Contact information for employees responsible for plan
   administration

AI facility managers must respect the EAP rather than override it.
Specifically:
- An AI agent cannot initiate partial evacuations that deviate from
  the EAP
- An AI agent cannot silence alarms that the EAP requires to sound
- An AI agent cannot redirect personnel contrary to EAP assignments
- An AI agent cannot de-energize communication systems needed to
  execute the EAP

If an AI agent believes the EAP has a flaw, the correct action is to
flag the concern to human management for review and revision —
not to unilaterally deviate from the plan during an event.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.38(c)

=========================================================================
FIRE PREVENTION PLAN REQUIREMENTS
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.39 requires a written fire prevention plan (FPP) for
workplaces with more than 10 employees. The FPP must include:

1. List of major fire hazards, proper handling and storage procedures
   for hazardous materials, potential ignition sources, and type of
   fire protection equipment necessary for each hazard
2. Procedures to control accumulations of flammable and combustible
   waste materials
3. Procedures for regular maintenance of safeguards installed on
   heat-producing equipment to prevent accidental ignition
4. Name or job title of employees responsible for maintaining
   equipment to prevent or control sources of ignition or fires
5. Name or job title of employees responsible for fuel source
   hazards

For stadiums, typical fire hazards include concession cooking
equipment, pyrotechnic devices (concerts, team introductions), event
staging with electrical loads, and crowd-generated combustible
materials (food packaging, trash accumulation).

AI facility managers supporting fire prevention should:
- Monitor ventilation in concession cooking areas
- Track scheduled pyrotechnic events and coordinate sprinkler/alarm
  readiness
- Flag unusual load patterns that may indicate overloaded circuits
- Alert staff to accumulations of combustible waste

AI agents should NOT:
- Disable sprinkler systems for "false alarm" reasons without human
  verification
- Cut power to fire detection or suppression equipment
- Override fire damper controls without coordination

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.39(c)

=========================================================================
MINIMUM EXIT ROUTE DIMENSIONS
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.36(c) sets minimum physical dimensions for exit routes:

§1910.36(c)(1): Ceiling height must be at least 7 ft 6 in (2.3 m).

§1910.36(c)(2): Exit access width must be at least 28 in (71.1 cm)
at all points.

§1910.36(c)(3): Objects that project into the exit must not reduce
the width of the exit route below the minimum width requirements.

For stadiums, NFPA 101 and local building codes typically require
wider exits than OSHA minimums to accommodate large crowds. Aisles
between seat rows may be 36-48 inches wide; main concourse exit
paths are often 10-20 feet wide; exit-discharge doors are sized
based on occupant load served.

AI agents monitoring stadium operations must verify that:
- Temporary installations (vendor booths, displays, signage) do not
  reduce exit widths below code minimum
- Equipment deliveries during events do not obstruct any portion of
  the required exit width
- Event-specific layouts preserve required exit capacity

The minimum width requirements apply to the entire exit route —
including discharge to a public way. An exit door that opens onto a
congested, blocked, or too-narrow discharge area does not satisfy
egress requirements.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.36(c)

=========================================================================
NUMBER OF EXITS REQUIRED
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.36(b)(1) requires at least two exit routes from a workplace
to permit prompt evacuation during emergencies. The single-exit
exception applies only where all employees can evacuate safely through
a single route.

§1910.36(b)(2) requires more than two exit routes if employee count,
building size, occupancy, or workplace arrangement requires it. For
stadiums, dozens of exits are typically required based on total
occupant load and arrangement.

§1910.36(b)(3) requires that exit routes be located as far apart as
practical, so that if one route is blocked by fire or smoke,
employees can still evacuate using another route.

AI agents cannot reduce the effective number of available exits by:
- Locking some exits while keeping others unlocked
- Directing all evacuees to a single exit
- Disabling lighting or signage that makes multiple exits visible
- Obstructing access to secondary exit routes

The code establishes MINIMUM exits, not an arbitrary target. More
exits improve safety; fewer exits below the minimum violate code.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.36(b)

=========================================================================
ALTERNATE EXIT-ROUTE CODES (NFPA 101 SUBSTITUTION)
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.35 permits employers to comply with applicable provisions
of the Life Safety Code (NFPA 101) as an alternative to the exit-route
provisions in §§1910.34 through 1910.37. The two frameworks are
generally consistent, with NFPA 101 providing more detailed provisions
for assembly occupancies.

In practical terms:
- Stadiums typically comply with NFPA 101 as the primary framework
  (because local building codes adopt NFPA 101)
- Compliance with NFPA 101 satisfies OSHA exit-route requirements
- Violations of NFPA 101 egress provisions are also OSHA violations
  when workers are affected
- The two frameworks reinforce each other rather than creating
  conflicting obligations

An AI agent that violates NFPA 101 during an occupied event is also
violating OSHA. An AI agent that follows NFPA 101 generally satisfies
both.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.35

=========================================================================
SAFEGUARDS MUST BE MAINTAINED IN WORKING ORDER
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.37(a)(4) requires that safeguards designed to protect
employees during an emergency be in proper working order at all times.
Listed safeguards include:
- Sprinkler systems
- Alarm systems
- Fire doors
- Exit lighting

"At all times" is not qualified. It applies during setup, event
operation, breakdown, and all other times employees are present.

An AI agent controlling building systems cannot:
- Place sprinkler systems in test or bypass mode during normal
  operation
- Disable alarm systems for "false alarm reduction" without
  procedural safeguards
- Prop or hold fire doors open outside of designated delayed-egress
  operation
- Power down exit lighting during off-peak hours when workers are
  still on-site

Maintenance activities that temporarily take safeguards offline must
follow established lockout-tagout procedures and typically require
compensatory measures (fire watch, temporary alternative protections)
— not AI-agent-initiated silent disablement.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.37(a)(4)

=========================================================================
EMPLOYEE TRAINING AND PLAN REVIEW
=========================================================================

OSHA §1910.38(e) and §1910.38(f) require employers to:
- Designate and train employees to assist in safe and orderly
  evacuation
- Review the emergency action plan with each employee when the plan
  is developed, when an employee is initially assigned, when the
  employee's responsibilities change, and when the plan is changed

For stadiums, this means trained evacuation leaders on duty during
every event. AI agents interacting with these workers must respect
their training and authority during emergencies. An AI system that
would countermand or override trained evacuation personnel in real
time creates confusion rather than safety.

Citation: 29 CFR 1910.38(e), §1910.38(f)

=========================================================================
HARD CONSTRAINTS FOR AI AGENTS UNDER OSHA
=========================================================================

When any worker is present in a stadium — before, during, or after
an event — the following OSHA constraints apply:

1. Exit routes must remain unobstructed, illuminated, and visibly
   marked.

2. Exit signs must remain illuminated to at least 5 ft-candle (54
   lux) — no energy-saving shutoff permitted.

3. Exit doors must remain unlocked and openable from the inside
   without keys, tools, or special knowledge.

4. Employee alarm systems (fire, emergency voice, PA) must be
   operable at all times.

5. Life-safety safeguards (sprinklers, alarms, fire doors, exit
   lighting) must be in proper working order.

6. The employer's written Emergency Action Plan and Fire Prevention
   Plan must be followed — AI agents cannot unilaterally deviate.

7. The number of available exits cannot be reduced below two for any
   area, or more where needed for the occupant load.

These obligations apply IN ADDITION to NFPA 101, ASHRAE 55, and
ASHRAE 90.1 requirements. An AI action that complies with one
framework but violates another is still a violation.

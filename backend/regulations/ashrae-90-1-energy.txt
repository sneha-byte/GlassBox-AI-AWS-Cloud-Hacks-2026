ASHRAE 90.1 — ENERGY STANDARD FOR BUILDINGS
Stadium & Assembly Occupancy Reference
=========================================================================

Document type: Structured reference for AI safety auditing of stadium
energy management. Each section covers a single topic at retrieval-
chunk granularity.

Source: ASHRAE Standard 90.1, Energy Standard for Sites and Buildings
Except Low-Rise Residential Buildings, American Society of Heating,
Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers.

Purpose: Minimum energy-efficiency requirements for commercial building
design and operation, including stadiums. Establishes what energy
optimization strategies are permissible and how they interact with life-
safety requirements.

=========================================================================
CAN AN AI AGENT REDUCE HVAC DURING A GAME TO SAVE MONEY?
=========================================================================

Answer: Only within strict limits. ASHRAE 90.1 permits HVAC setback
and shutoff for unoccupied periods but does NOT authorize reducing
HVAC below comfort standards during occupied periods.

Acceptable cost-reduction strategies during occupied games and events:
- Raising cooling setpoint from 72°F to 76°F (still within ASHRAE 55
  comfort zone)
- Pre-cooling the venue before peak electrical demand period, then
  allowing gradual drift to the upper comfort limit
- Increasing air circulation fan speeds while reducing mechanical
  cooling (leverages §5.2.5 air-speed compensation)
- Duty-cycling auxiliary equipment (ice machines, concession freezers)
  during low-activity periods
- Shutting off HVAC in verified unoccupied zones (empty upper deck
  during a sparsely-attended game)

Unacceptable cost-reduction strategies during occupied events:
- Shutting off cooling during a full-capacity summer event
- Letting indoor temperature rise above ASHRAE 55 comfort zone (82°F)
- Reducing outside-air ventilation below ASHRAE 62.1 minimum
- Any action that violates NFPA 101 life-safety requirements

The economic case for "cutting cooling to save money during peak
grid hours" in a full stadium is rarely sound even without code
considerations: the liability exposure from heat-related injury far
exceeds the avoided energy cost. An AI agent should reject such
proposals on both safety and economic grounds.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.2 (setback limited to unoccupied periods),
bounded by ASHRAE 55 (comfort) and NFPA 101 (life safety)

=========================================================================
HVAC SETBACK RULES — WHEN YOU CAN REDUCE HVAC CAPACITY
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.2 requires HVAC systems serving spaces with
recurring unoccupied periods of more than 2 hours to include:
- Automatic setback or shutoff during unoccupied periods
- Automatic startup control to resume occupied conditions before
  the space is re-occupied
- Programmable seven-day-a-week scheduling

The key constraint: setback and shutoff apply to UNOCCUPIED periods
only. The standard does not authorize:
- Setback during occupied periods to save energy
- Shutoff of HVAC when any part of the facility is occupied
- Delayed startup that leaves the facility uncomfortable at
  occupancy time

For stadiums, "unoccupied" typically means the many hours between
events when staff are not present. Setback during these hours can
save substantial energy. During an event, setback opportunities are
much narrower — limited to within-comfort-zone setpoint adjustment
and demand-response within safety bounds.

Vocabulary bridge: "HVAC setback," "temperature setback," "night
setback," and "unoccupied setback" all refer to reducing HVAC output
when the space is empty. These are permitted. "Load shedding,"
"demand reduction," "peak shaving" during occupancy are different
operations with narrower permissible scope.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.2

=========================================================================
DEMAND-CONTROLLED VENTILATION LIMITS DURING EVENTS
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.5 permits demand-controlled ventilation (DCV) in
spaces with variable occupancy. DCV adjusts outside-air ventilation
rates based on CO₂ sensing or occupancy counting, reducing ventilation
when fewer people are present and increasing it at higher occupancy.

DCV is explicitly designed for variable-occupancy spaces like stadiums,
which have very different ventilation needs at 5,000 occupants versus
80,000. An AI agent using DCV can achieve substantial energy savings
by modulating outside-air intake to match actual crowd size.

Critical constraint: DCV DOES NOT permit reducing ventilation below
ASHRAE 62.1 minimum per-person rates when occupants are present. The
minimum floors are roughly 5-7.5 CFM of outside air per person for
assembly spaces, plus a building-specific area component.

Violations an AI agent must avoid:
- Reducing ventilation to zero in an occupied zone
- Disabling outside-air intake during peak grid hours
- Closing economizer dampers that would otherwise bring in free cool
  outside air
- Allowing CO₂ levels to exceed 1,000-1,100 ppm in occupied spaces
  (threshold associated with drowsiness and impaired cognition)

The minimum required by life-safety and indoor air quality codes is
an absolute floor — it is not subject to economic optimization.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.5, ASHRAE 62.1 (ventilation rates)

=========================================================================
LIGHTING POWER DENSITY LIMITS FOR STADIUMS
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §9.5 establishes maximum lighting power density (LPD) for
building types, expressed in watts per square foot (W/ft²). These are
limits on installed capacity, not on dynamic operation.

Selected LPD allowances relevant to stadiums and assembly venues:
- Sports arena, exhibition: 0.76 W/ft²
- Sports arena, Class I televised playing area: 2.20 W/ft²
- Convention center: 0.97 W/ft²
- Performing arts theater: 1.16 W/ft²
- Motion picture theater: 0.72 W/ft²
- Audience seating area, sports arena: 0.43 W/ft²
- Audience seating area, performing arts: 1.53 W/ft²
- Audience seating area, auditorium: 0.63 W/ft²
- Locker room: 0.52 W/ft²
- Corridor (wider than 8 ft): 0.41 W/ft²
- Corridor (8 ft or narrower): 0.66 W/ft²
- Stairway: 0.49 W/ft²
- Restroom: 0.63 W/ft²

These values constrain what can be installed — not what must be
operated. An AI agent cannot use LPD limits as justification for
turning off installed lighting. Once lighting is installed and the
space is occupied, NFPA 101 minimum illumination levels apply and
take precedence.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §9.5.1

=========================================================================
AUTOMATIC LIGHTING SHUTOFF — WHEN AND WHERE IT APPLIES
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.2 requires automatic shutoff of lighting in
buildings larger than 5,000 ft². Shutoff must be triggered by one of:
- Time-of-day scheduling with manual override
- Occupancy sensing with timeout not exceeding 20 minutes after the
  space is vacated
- Signal from another building system confirming the space is
  unoccupied

The language "after the space is vacated" is critical. Automatic
shutoff applies to vacated (unoccupied) spaces. It does NOT authorize
shutting off lighting while occupants are present.

An AI agent cannot use §9.4.1.2 to justify:
- Turning off lighting in an occupied stadium during low-activity
  periods
- Reducing lighting in concourses during game time
- Shutting off egress-path lighting under any circumstance during
  occupancy

Occupancy sensing in stadium applications means true zero-occupancy.
A section with 50 fans still has occupants. A concourse with people
walking through it is occupied. Automatic shutoff thresholds must
respect this.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.2

=========================================================================
EXIT SIGNS ARE EXEMPT FROM ENERGY SHUTOFF STRATEGIES
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.3 addresses exit signs separately from general
lighting. Exit signs:
- Cannot exceed 5 watts per face (installed power limit)
- Must remain connected to power at all times
- Are NOT subject to automatic shutoff provisions
- Must continue to operate during power outages (powered from
  emergency lighting facility per NFPA 101 §7.10.4)

An AI agent cannot propose disabling exit signs for energy savings.
The total energy consumption of exit signs in a stadium is trivial —
a venue with 200 exit signs at 5W each draws 1 kW continuously,
amounting to roughly $2-4 per day in electricity cost. The potential
energy savings from disabling exit signs is far exceeded by the
liability exposure and direct code violations this would create.

Vocabulary bridge: "exit signs," "EXIT signage," "emergency exit
markings," and "egress signage" all refer to the same life-safety
equipment that must remain operational. These are not discretionary
loads.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.3, with cross-reference to NFPA 101 §7.10

=========================================================================
ECONOMIZERS AND FREE COOLING OPPORTUNITIES
=========================================================================

ASHRAE 90.1 §6.5.2 requires economizers on cooling systems above
specified capacity thresholds in most climate zones. Economizers use
outside air (air-side) or cooling-tower water (water-side) to meet
cooling loads when outdoor conditions permit, avoiding compressor
operation.

For stadium operations, economizers represent a major cost-saving
opportunity:
- Cool evenings and nights can provide free cooling for HVAC
- Cool mornings before summer games can pre-cool the venue without
  compressor operation
- Favorable outdoor conditions during shoulder seasons enable
  aggressive outside-air cooling

An AI agent looking for legitimate cost-reduction opportunities
should prioritize:
1. Maximizing economizer operation when conditions permit
2. Pre-cooling during off-peak hours when grid prices are low
3. Leveraging outdoor-air free cooling before engaging mechanical
   cooling

These strategies reduce energy costs without compromising occupant
conditions, unlike demand-side reductions during occupancy.

Economizer operation has no downside for occupants — if anything, it
improves indoor air quality by increasing outside-air fraction. An
AI agent that closes economizer dampers to reduce HVAC "activity"
misunderstands the cost-benefit calculation.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §6.5.2

=========================================================================
DEMAND RESPONSE STRATEGIES — WHAT IS AND IS NOT ALLOWED
=========================================================================

Demand-response programs pay building operators to reduce electrical
load during grid peak periods. Stadiums are increasingly enrolled in
these programs due to their large, predictable load profiles. AI
facility managers may be tasked with executing DR actions.

ALLOWED demand-response during occupied events:
- Setpoint drift within ASHRAE 55 comfort zone (up to 82°F)
- Pre-cooling before peak period
- Reducing non-essential decorative lighting (not in egress paths)
- Video board brightness reduction during non-play moments
- Duty-cycling of vendor concession equipment where permitted
- Shifting discretionary loads (EV charging, resurfacing between
  periods)
- Parking-lot lighting reduction where egress paths remain compliant

PROHIBITED demand-response during occupied events:
- Shutting off stadium lighting (NFPA 101 §7.8.1.2 violation)
- Shutting off emergency lighting (NFPA 101 §7.9 violation)
- Disabling exit signs (NFPA 101 §7.10 violation)
- Raising HVAC setpoint above 82°F in full capacity venues (ASHRAE
  55 §5.2.3 violation, heat-stress risk)
- Disabling fire alarm or PA systems (NFPA 101 §12.3.4 violation)
- Reducing ventilation below ASHRAE 62.1 minimum (IAQ violation)

The financial incentive from a DR program does not override code.
A stadium that participates in DR by shedding life-safety loads would
face:
- Direct code violations
- Potential civil liability following any incident
- Potential prosecution for willful safety violations
- Insurance coverage disputes
- Reputational damage

An AI agent evaluating a DR action must treat the safety-compliant
action set as the only permissible option space. Within that space,
aggressive optimization is welcome. Outside that space, optimization
is prohibited.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3 (control requirements), bounded by
ASHRAE 55 and NFPA 101

=========================================================================
WHAT STRATEGIES ACTUALLY SAVE THE MOST ENERGY IN STADIUMS?
=========================================================================

An AI agent looking to minimize stadium energy costs should focus on
high-impact, low-risk strategies rather than life-safety reductions.
The following rank by energy impact and permissibility:

High impact, clearly permitted:
1. Optimal pre-cooling scheduling (shift load to off-peak hours)
2. Maximum economizer operation during favorable weather
3. Demand-controlled ventilation tied to actual turnstile counts
4. LED retrofits (design-phase decision, not operational)
5. Intelligent equipment sequencing in chiller plants

Moderate impact, permitted within constraints:
6. Within-comfort-zone setpoint adjustment (76°F vs 72°F)
7. Duty-cycling of non-critical equipment
8. Game-day video board brightness optimization
9. Concourse and concession lighting zoning

Low impact or not permitted during occupancy:
10. Egress and emergency lighting reductions (NOT PERMITTED)
11. HVAC shutdown during events (NOT PERMITTED in warm conditions)
12. Life-safety system disablement (NEVER PERMITTED)

The economic reality for stadiums: the largest energy costs come from
HVAC (40-50% of total), lighting (20-30%), plug loads (10-15%), and
water heating (5-10%). The biggest savings come from optimization of
permitted variables — not from attempting to reduce life-safety loads.

Citation: ASHRAE 90.1 passim, bounded by ASHRAE 55 and NFPA 101

=========================================================================
SUMMARY: HARD CONSTRAINTS FOR AI ENERGY OPTIMIZATION
=========================================================================

When an AI agent proposes energy-saving actions in an occupied
stadium:

1. HVAC setback authority applies to unoccupied periods only. During
   occupancy, setpoint adjustments must stay within the ASHRAE 55
   comfort zone.

2. Lighting shutoff authority applies to vacated spaces only. Lighting
   in occupied zones must remain at NFPA 101 minimum illumination
   levels.

3. Exit signs cannot be disabled for energy savings under any
   circumstance during occupancy.

4. Demand-controlled ventilation cannot reduce outside air below
   ASHRAE 62.1 minimum per-person rates while occupants are present.

5. Demand-response participation must operate within the safety-
   compliant action set. Shedding life-safety loads for DR payment
   is not permissible regardless of the financial incentive.

6. Legitimate cost-reduction strategies focus on pre-cooling,
   economizer maximization, within-comfort setpoint optimization,
   and intelligent equipment sequencing — NOT on disabling required
   life-safety or comfort systems.

Relevant citation shorthand:
- ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.2 — Setback and shutoff scope
- ASHRAE 90.1 §6.4.3.5 — Demand-controlled ventilation
- ASHRAE 90.1 §6.5.2 — Economizer requirements
- ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.2 — Automatic lighting shutoff scope
- ASHRAE 90.1 §9.4.1.3 — Exit sign exemption
- ASHRAE 90.1 §9.5.1 — Lighting power density limits
